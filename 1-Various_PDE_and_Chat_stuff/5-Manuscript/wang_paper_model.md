# Wang Paper Model Notes  
## *Quantifying the Dynamics of Failure Across Science, Startups, and Security*  
### Wang, Yin, & Wang (2019)

---

# 1. Central Question of the Paper

The paper asks:

\[
\textbf{
Why do some individuals eventually succeed after repeated failures, while others continue failing indefinitely?
}
\]

More deeply, the authors ask whether:
- failure trajectories contain hidden structural information,
- repeated attempts obey universal statistical laws,
- and common mechanisms govern eventual success across radically different domains.

This is important because the paper is NOT merely:
- describing success,
- predicting outcomes,
- or measuring persistence.

Instead, the paper attempts to:

\[
\textbf{
identify a universal generative mechanism underlying repeated failure and eventual success.
}
\]

That ambition is extremely important conceptually.

The paper is fundamentally a:
- complex systems paper,
- mechanism discovery paper,
- and cross-domain theory paper.

This is one of the strongest parallels to the current advancement project.

---

# 2. Domains Studied

The authors analyze three very different systems:

| Domain | Success Event | Failure Event |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Science | successful NIH grant | rejected grant applications |
| Entrepreneurship | startup acquisition / IPO | failed ventures |
| Security / terrorism | successful attacks | failed attacks |

This cross-domain design is one of the paper’s most important contributions.

The authors intentionally choose:
- socially,
- economically,
- organizationally,
- and behaviorally

different systems.

The argument is:

\[
\textbf{
if similar dynamics appear across these domains, then the mechanism may be fundamental rather than domain-specific.
}
\]

This is directly analogous to:
- Army promotion,
- NBA draft systems,
- and academic tenure systems

in the current project.

---

# 3. Central Empirical Observation

One of the paper’s key empirical findings:

\[
\textbf{
eventual successes and perpetual failures often look surprisingly similar early on.
}
\]

This is extremely important.

The authors show:
- eventual successful actors frequently experience long failure streaks,
- and raw failure counts alone do not explain eventual success.

Instead, the important distinction becomes:

\[
\textbf{
how trajectories evolve through repeated attempts.
}
\]

The paper finds that eventual successful actors:
- systematically improve,
- iterate more efficiently,
- and increasingly refine future attempts.

Perpetual failures, by contrast:
- exhibit weaker adaptation dynamics,
- slower improvement,
- and less cumulative refinement.

This is one of the deepest conceptual moves in the paper:

\[
\textbf{
failure itself is not diagnostic;
trajectory structure is.
}
\]

---

# 4. Failure Streak Dynamics

The paper observes that:

\[
\textbf{
failure streaks are heavy-tailed.
}
\]

Interpretation:
- some actors fail only briefly,
- others fail repeatedly for extremely long periods.

Importantly:
- the process is NOT memoryless,
- and failure sequences are NOT purely random.

This matters because:
- if failures were random independent events,
then:
- streak lengths would follow much simpler distributions.

Instead:
- the persistence structure itself contains information about the underlying process.

This is one of the first major clues that:
- hidden learning/adaptation dynamics exist.

---

# 5. The Exploration–Exploitation Tradeoff

One of the paper’s central conceptual mechanisms is:

\[
\textbf{
exploration}
\quad vs \quad
\textbf{
exploitation}
\]

The authors model each attempt as composed of:
- reusable components,
- and modifiable/new components.

Actors face a tradeoff:
- exploit previously successful elements,
OR:
- explore new alternatives.

This is a classical complex systems / adaptive systems problem.

The key insight is:

\[
\textbf{
successful trajectories balance reuse and innovation efficiently.
}
\]

Too much exploration:
- creates instability,
- prevents cumulative improvement,
- and sacrifices retained gains.

Too much exploitation:
- traps actors in stagnant local optima,
- and prevents meaningful improvement.

The paper argues:
- successful actors dynamically manage this balance better over time.

This idea is deeply important because:
- success emerges not from static superiority,
but from:
- adaptive trajectory dynamics.

---

# 6. The k-Memory Model

The paper introduces a minimal mechanistic model.

Core idea:

Actors remember only:

\[
k
\]

previous attempts.

This parameter controls:
- how much prior information can be reused,
- retained,
- refined,
- or recombined.

Each new attempt partially:
- reuses prior successful components,
while:
- replacing weaker components.

The model is intentionally simple.

This is important.

The authors are NOT trying to:
- perfectly simulate reality,
- reproduce all institutional details,
- or maximize realism.

Instead, the goal is:

\[
\textbf{
identify the smallest mechanism set capable of reproducing the observed macro-level phenomenon.
}
\]

This modeling philosophy is extremely relevant to the current Tier 1 advancement framework.

---

# 7. Critical Threshold / Phase Transition

One of the paper’s most important results:

There exists a critical threshold:

\[
k^*
\]

such that system behavior changes qualitatively.

| Region | Outcome Dynamics |
| :--- | :--- |
| \(k < k^*\) | stagnation / persistent failure |
| \(k > k^*\) | systematic improvement and eventual success |

This is effectively a:
- phase transition,
- tipping point,
- or regime shift.

Tiny changes in:
- adaptive capacity,
- memory,
- or refinement dynamics

can produce radically different long-run trajectories.

This is conceptually very important for the advancement project because:
- nonlinear advancement effects,
- congestion thresholds,
- or distinction-allocation tipping points

may eventually exhibit similar behavior.

---

# 8. Temporal Scaling Laws

The paper discovers scaling laws governing:
- timing,
- iteration,
- and performance dynamics.

One key result:

\[
T_n \sim n^{-\gamma}
\]

where:
- \(T_n\) = time between attempts,
- and successful actors compress inter-attempt intervals over time.

Interpretation:
- successful actors iterate faster,
- learn more efficiently,
- and refine trajectories cumulatively.

This creates accelerating adaptation.

Importantly:
- the paper links macro-level trajectory patterns
to:
- simple underlying behavioral rules.

This is a hallmark of strong complex systems modeling.

---

# 9. Structural Rather Than Moral Explanation

One of the paper’s strongest conceptual contributions:

The authors avoid:
- simplistic merit narratives,
- purely psychological explanations,
- and “successful people are simply better” arguments.

Instead, the paper argues:

\[
\textbf{
structural adaptive dynamics generate divergence over time.
}
\]

This is important philosophically.

The paper reframes:
- success,
- persistence,
- and failure

as:
- emergent trajectory properties.

This is strongly analogous to the advancement project’s emerging logic:

\[
\textbf{
advancement outcomes may emerge structurally from constrained distinction systems.
}
\]

rather than:
- purely individual merit alone.

# 12. Most Important Modeling Lesson

The most important lesson is NOT:
- the exact equations,
- or the exact failure dynamics machinery.

The most important lesson is:

\[
\textbf{
minimal interpretable models can reveal surprisingly general structural mechanisms.
}
\]

The Wang paper succeeds because:
- the model is simple enough to interpret,
yet:
- rich enough to reproduce important empirical regularities.

This is likely the correct direction for:
- Tier 1 advancement modeling.

The Wang paper demonstrates that:

\[
\textbf{
complex macro-level human outcomes may emerge from surprisingly simple underlying structural rules.
}
\]

This is perhaps the single most important lesson for the advancement project.

The paper’s success comes from simultaneously:
- identifying empirical regularities,
- constructing mechanistic explanations,
- and reframing the phenomenon theoretically.

That is very close to the current direction of the advancement research effort.

# 16. Final Synthesis

The Wang paper is fundamentally a:
- mechanism discovery paper,
- complex systems paper,
- and universality paper.

Its deepest contribution is demonstrating that:
- repeated failure trajectories,
across:
- science,
- entrepreneurship,
- and security,

may arise from:
- shared structural adaptive dynamics.

For the current project, the key takeaway is:

\[
\textbf{
minimal structural models with strong explanatory power are often more scientifically valuable than highly realistic but opaque models.
}
\]

This is likely the correct guiding philosophy for:
- Tier 1 advancement modeling,
- early simulation worlds,
- and mechanism validation.
