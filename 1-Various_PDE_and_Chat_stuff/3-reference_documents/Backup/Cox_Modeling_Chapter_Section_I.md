# Section I: Survival Analysis: The Foundation

## A. The Time-to-Event Framework: Why Standard Methods Fail

When analyzing how organizations identify and promote talent, a fundamental challenge emerges: how do we model the timing of career milestones when traditional regression methods fail to account for the complex temporal structure of careers? This question lies at the heart of survival analysis, a class of statistical methods originally developed in medical research to study patient outcomes such as time until death, disease recurrence, or treatment failure. The terminology reflects these origins—terms like "survival function," "hazard function," and "failure time" may seem incongruous when applied to positive career events like promotion. However, the mathematical framework is perfectly suited to any time-to-event analysis, regardless of whether the event is desirable (promotion) or undesirable (death, disease). The "survival" function simply represents the probability of not yet experiencing the event, and the "hazard" function captures the instantaneous rate at which events occur.

The analysis of military officer promotion timing fundamentally represents a time-to-event problem. In this framework, the outcome of interest is not simply whether an event occurs, but rather *when* it occurs relative to a meaningful starting point. For the study of officer careers, the primary outcome of interest is **time-in-grade**—the duration an officer serves at a particular rank before promotion to the next level. The event of interest is **promotion to Major**, a critical career milestone that represents both recognition of performance and eligibility for increased responsibility and leadership roles. The time scale for this analysis is measured in days, months, or years from the officer's **Date of Rank (DOR) as Captain**—the point at which they were promoted to the rank of Captain. This starting point establishes a common baseline for all officers, allowing for meaningful comparisons of promotion timing across individuals with different career trajectories.

The time-to-event framework is particularly well-suited for understanding military promotion systems because it naturally accommodates the temporal dynamics of career progression. Unlike cross-sectional analyses that capture only a snapshot of officer status at a single point in time, survival analysis allows us to model the entire career trajectory, accounting for the fact that officers may be observed at different stages of their careers and may experience the event of interest (promotion) at different times, or not at all during the observation period. This capability is essential when analyzing large-scale administrative data, such as our dataset of 201,038 officers observed over 24 years, where officers enter and exit the observation period at different times and may experience promotion at various points in their careers.

It is important to note that when we use the term "risk" in this context, its meaning differs from common usage. In medical contexts, "risk" typically refers to the probability of an unfavorable outcome (e.g., risk of death, risk of disease). In our promotion framework, however, the "risk" of promotion is actually a positive outcome—it represents the probability or intensity of a desirable event. When we discuss competing events (also called "competing risks" in the survival analysis literature), we are referring to multiple mutually exclusive outcomes that can terminate the observation period. In our context, these competing events include promotion (a positive outcome) and attrition (which, from the Army's perspective regarding promotion, could be considered a negative outcome, as it represents the loss of an officer from the promotion pool). This methodological adaptation from medical research to labor economics and organizational studies is well-established, though it requires careful consideration of domain-specific complexities, such as competing events, that may not have been central to the original medical applications.

## B. Key Concepts: Survival Function, Hazard Function, and Censoring

### The Survival Function: Tracking Who Remains Unpromoted

The **survival function**, denoted S(t), represents the probability that an individual has not yet experienced the event of interest by time t. In the context of military officer promotion, S(t) is the probability that an officer has not been promoted to Major by time t since their promotion to Captain. Formally:

$$S(t) = P(T > t)$$

where T is the time until promotion.

Intuitively, the survival function answers the question: "What proportion of officers remain unpromoted by time t?" The survival function has several important properties: it is a non-increasing function that starts at S(0) = 1 (all officers begin unpromoted) and approaches zero as time increases (eventually, all officers who will be promoted will have been promoted, assuming no competing events). The shape of the survival function provides insights into the distribution of promotion times—a steep decline indicates many early promotions, while a gradual decline suggests more uniform timing or later promotions. In the context of military promotion, where structured timelines create natural promotion windows (Below Zone, Primary Zone), the survival function may exhibit characteristic patterns that reflect these institutional structures.

### The Hazard Function: The Instantaneous Rate of Promotion

The **hazard function**, denoted h(t), represents the instantaneous rate at which events occur at time t, given that the individual has survived (not yet experienced the event) up to time t. In promotion research, h(t) captures the "promotion intensity" at any given point in an officer's career. Formally:

$$h(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{P(t \leq T < t + \Delta t \mid T \geq t)}{\Delta t}$$

The hazard function provides a dynamic view of promotion intensity that complements the survival function. While the survival function answers "What proportion of officers remain unpromoted by time t?", the hazard function answers "Among officers still unpromoted at time t, how likely are they to be promoted in the next instant?" This instantaneous perspective is particularly valuable for understanding how promotion probabilities change over the course of an officer's career, potentially revealing periods of heightened or diminished promotion activity. In military promotion systems, the hazard function should theoretically reflect the structured promotion windows: low before the Below Zone window, higher during the Below Zone period, highest during the Primary Zone, and lower after the Primary Zone for those still eligible.

### Right-Censoring: Learning from Incomplete Information

**Right-censoring** occurs when an individual has not yet experienced the event of interest by the end of the observation period. In military officer research, right-censoring is common because many officers remain in service at the conclusion of the study period without having been promoted to Major. These officers provide valuable information—we know they survived (remained unpromoted) for the duration of their observed service, even though we do not know their ultimate promotion outcome.

Standard regression methods would typically exclude or mishandle these censored observations, treating them as missing data or incorrectly assuming they will never be promoted. Survival analysis methods, however, appropriately incorporate this partial information, recognizing that an officer who is right-censored while still in their Below Zone window provides different information than an officer who is right-censored after passing through their Primary Zone without promotion (who remains eligible but with dramatically reduced promotion probability), but both contribute meaningfully to understanding promotion timing. This efficient use of information is particularly important in military research, where censoring rates can be substantial. Officers may leave service for a variety of reasons: some may leave for reasons unrelated to promotion potential (such as family considerations or personal circumstances), while others may recognize that they are unlikely to be promoted and choose to leave before being formally passed over—a tendency that may increase at higher ranks. Still others may leave precisely because they recognize their high potential for promotion and see greater opportunities elsewhere that offer more meaningful or lucrative prospects without the rigid promotion timelines of military service. Regardless of the reason for attrition, their career trajectories up to the point of departure contain valuable information about promotion processes.

### Competing Events: When Multiple Outcomes Are Possible

A critical complexity in military officer research is the presence of **competing events** (also referred to as "competing risks" in the survival analysis literature)—multiple mutually exclusive events that can terminate the observation period. In this context, officers may experience one of two primary outcomes: promotion to Major or attrition from the service (through retirement, separation, or other forms of exit). These events are mutually exclusive and represent fundamentally different career outcomes.

Competing events require specialized analytical approaches because the occurrence of one event (e.g., attrition) precludes the observation of the other (e.g., promotion). Standard survival analysis methods that treat attrition as simple censoring can produce biased estimates, as they implicitly assume that censored individuals (those who attrit) would have the same promotion hazard as those who remain in service. Competing events models explicitly account for the fact that officers who leave the service have a different underlying promotion probability structure than those who remain, allowing for more accurate estimation of promotion hazards. This complexity means that many standard survival analysis software packages, which were developed primarily for single-event scenarios common in medical research, may not adequately handle competing events without additional methodological considerations or specialized implementations.

### Left-Truncation: Accounting for Delayed Entry

**Left-truncation** occurs when individuals enter the study at different points in their "survival time." In military officer research, this requires careful consideration of promotion eligibility windows. Military promotion systems operate on structured timelines, with officers becoming eligible for promotion only during specific periods: the "Below Zone" (BZ) window for early promotion and the "Primary Zone" (PZ) window for standard promotion eligibility.

Left-truncation occurs when an officer's promotion eligibility window (BZ or PZ) has already begun before the study observation period starts. These officers have already "survived" (remained unpromoted) during a period when they were eligible for promotion, and this pre-observation survival time must be accounted for in the analysis. Importantly, an officer whose Date of Rank as Captain (DOR CPT) predates the study period but whose eligibility window begins during or after the study period is *not* left-truncated—they have not yet had the opportunity to be promoted, so their observation period begins at the start of their eligibility window, not at their DOR CPT.

Left-truncation requires careful handling because it can introduce selection bias if not properly addressed. Officers who have already entered their eligibility window before the study begins represent a selected sample—they are, by definition, officers who have not yet been promoted despite having had the opportunity during their eligibility period. Survival analysis methods can accommodate left-truncation by conditioning on the fact that individuals have already survived to their entry time (the start of the study period, given that they were already eligible), ensuring that estimates reflect the appropriate risk sets and do not incorrectly attribute survival time before eligibility to the promotion hazard.

## C. Why Standard Regression Methods Fail for Time-to-Event Data

### The Censoring Problem: Incomplete Information Cannot Be Ignored

Standard regression methods, including ordinary least squares (OLS) and logistic regression, are fundamentally ill-suited for time-to-event data because they cannot appropriately handle **censored observations**. In OLS regression, for example, a censored observation (an officer who has not been promoted by the end of the study) would require an outcome value—but we do not know the true time-to-promotion for this officer. Treating censored observations as missing data and excluding them from analysis introduces severe selection bias, as it systematically removes officers with longer time-to-promotion from the sample. In our dataset of 201,038 officers, this would mean discarding information from potentially tens of thousands of officers who remain in service, dramatically reducing the precision of our estimates and potentially introducing systematic bias.

Logistic regression, while designed for binary outcomes, similarly fails because it requires complete information about whether the event occurred. A logistic model predicting "promoted vs. not promoted" would incorrectly treat an officer who has not been promoted after one year of observation the same as an officer who has not been promoted after ten years, despite the dramatically different information these observations provide about promotion likelihood. This approach discards the temporal dimension that is central to understanding career progression.

### The Timing Problem: When Matters as Much as Whether

Even if censoring were not an issue, standard regression methods fail to capture the essential temporal dimension of time-to-event data. These methods treat time as either a covariate (in OLS) or ignore it entirely (in logistic regression), rather than recognizing time as the fundamental outcome of interest. This approach loses critical information about *when* events occur, which is often as important as *whether* they occur.

For example, finding that prestige unit assignment is correlated with promotion is valuable, but finding that it is correlated with *earlier* promotion provides much richer insights into career dynamics. Survival analysis methods preserve this temporal information by modeling the entire time-to-event distribution, not just the binary occurrence of the event. This capability is essential for understanding how factors influence not just the probability of promotion, but the timing of promotion—a distinction that is central to identifying factors associated with accelerated career progression.

### Time-Varying Covariates: Capturing Dynamic Career Trajectories

Military officer careers are characterized by **time-varying covariates**—variables whose values change over time for the same individual. An officer's cumulative performance evaluations, unit assignments, and other career characteristics evolve throughout their service, and these changes may influence promotion timing. Standard regression methods typically require that covariates remain constant over the observation period, or they must resort to ad-hoc approaches like using only baseline values or time-averaged measures, both of which discard valuable information about how changing circumstances affect promotion intensity.

Survival analysis methods, particularly the Cox proportional hazards model, are specifically designed to accommodate time-varying covariates, allowing the model to update covariate values as officers progress through their careers. Importantly, these methods can include both static variables (such as demographic characteristics or year group) and time-varying variables (such as cumulative performance metrics or current unit assignments) in the same model, allowing for comprehensive analysis of factors influencing promotion timing. This capability is essential for accurately modeling the dynamic nature of military careers, where an officer's promotion hazard may change as their performance record accumulates or as they move between different types of assignments, while also accounting for stable characteristics that may influence career trajectories.

### Information Loss: The Cost of Discarding Partial Information

Perhaps most critically, standard regression methods **ignore valuable information** contained in censored observations. An officer who has served as a Captain for eight years without promotion provides meaningful information about the lower tail of the promotion time distribution, even though we do not know their ultimate promotion outcome. Survival analysis methods use this partial information through the risk set concept—at any given time, the set of individuals "at risk" of experiencing the event includes both those who will eventually be promoted and those who will be censored, and both groups contribute to estimating the hazard at that time.

This efficient use of information is particularly important in military research, where censoring rates can be substantial. In our analysis, a significant proportion of officers may still be in service and awaiting promotion at the end of the 24-year observation period. Discarding this information, as standard methods do, would result in less precise estimates and potentially biased conclusions about factors influencing promotion timing. Survival analysis methods allow us to extract maximum information from the data, providing more powerful and accurate estimates of the relationships between various factors and promotion timing.
