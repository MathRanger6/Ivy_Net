# Business Dynamics Approach

## Overview

This document presents a **Business Dynamics / System Dynamics
(MIT-style)** reframing of the empirical finding---an inverted-U
relationship between promotion probability and peer pool quality---using
the language of **stocks, flows, feedback loops, delays, and path
dependence**.

The goal is to interpret the empirical pattern as an outcome of
**endogenous system structure**, where organizational evaluation
processes create nonlinear dynamics over time.

Throughout, we explicitly distinguish: - **Empirical result (observed,
non-causal)**\
- **System-dynamics interpretation (conceptual, mechanism-oriented)**

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# (a) Mechanism (Stock--Flow--Feedback Language)

From a system-dynamics perspective, the promotion system can be
conceptualized as a **multi-stage stock-and-flow system** in which
officers accumulate within organizational states and transition between
them through flows such as evaluation, promotion, and attrition. At any
point in time, officers reside in **stocks defined by their current
evaluation pool (senior rater × time)**, and their performance is
translated into signals that influence the **flow rate into the
promotion stock (Major)**.

The key empirical variable---mean top-block rate in a pool---captures
the **composition of the stock within a node**, which shapes the
effectiveness of the signal-generation process. When pool quality is low
to moderate, increases in average performance improve the **conversion
efficiency** of performance into promotion outcomes. In this regime, the
system behaves as though higher-quality local environments increase the
**throughflow rate** from the eligible stock to the promoted stock.

However, as pool quality becomes very high, **balancing feedback
mechanisms** become dominant. Promotion is governed by **capacity
constraints** (finite promotion slots), which limit the outflow from the
eligible stock. When many high-performing individuals accumulate in the
same pool, they collectively increase pressure on the promotion flow but
cannot all advance simultaneously. This produces **congestion within the
stock**, where the presence of many strong candidates reduces the
marginal flow probability for each individual. Over time, the
interaction between **reinforcing improvements in signal quality** and
**balancing constraints on capacity** generates a **nonlinear
response**, observed empirically as an inverted-U pattern. This
interpretation remains consistent with observational evidence and does
not imply causal peer effects.

# (b) Causal Loop Structure (Textual)

## Reinforcing Loop R1: Quality Amplification

Higher pool quality\
→ improved signal clarity\
→ higher promotion likelihood\
→ increased association of strong officers with strong pools\
→ higher pool quality

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Reinforcing Loop R2: Capability Development (Conceptual)

Higher pool quality\
→ stronger peer environment\
→ improved individual performance\
→ higher measured pool quality

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Balancing Loop B1: Capacity Constraint

Higher pool quality\
→ more high-performing officers in pool\
→ increased demand for promotion\
→ fixed promotion capacity binds\
→ reduced individual promotion probability

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Balancing Loop B2: Signal Crowding

Higher pool quality\
→ many similar high signals\
→ reduced distinguishability\
→ lower selection probability per individual

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## System Behavior

-   Low--moderate quality: reinforcing loops dominate → rising promotion
    rates\
-   High quality: balancing loops dominate → declining promotion rates

Result: **Inverted-U outcome emerges endogenously**

# Final Bridge

This framing shows that promotion outcomes can arise from
**feedback-driven system structure**, where evaluation environments both
improve signal quality and generate congestion. In management terms,
this implies that **organizational design shapes advancement through
dynamic feedback, not just static performance differences**, making
evaluation systems active components of talent allocation.
